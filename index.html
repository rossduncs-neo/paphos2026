Bars & restaurants
Where to actually go
Curated. Not TripAdvisor's top ten. These are the places worth the taxi fare.
The one to book first
7 St. Georges Tavern
★★★★★
Yeroskipou village · 10 min taxi · Book in advance · One sitting per night
No menu. No wine list. You sit down and the owner starts bringing food. Twenty-odd dishes over two hours — homegrown olives, kleftiko, wild greens, cheese pies, lamb, things you can't name but will eat anyway. Organic ingredients from their own farm. Homemade wine at €10 a litre. Absurdly good value.
Do not miss this. It is the dinner of the trip. Book before you land.
Old Town gem
Laona Restaurant
★★★★★
Paphos Old Town · Family-run · Locals eat here
The kind of place that has no reason to try hard because it doesn't need to. Rabbit stew, meatballs, hearty stews made from whatever arrived that morning. Warm, slightly chaotic, genuinely Cypriot. Multiple groups have gone three times in one trip. That tells you everything.
Go on a night when nobody can agree on anything. It solves the problem.
Hidden local
Mandra Tavern
★★★★★
Paphos Old Town · Stone walls + garden courtyard · Arrive before 7pm
Rustic. Tucked away from the main drag. Ingredients from the owner's family farm in the Troodos. Village-style grilled meats, seasonal vegetables, courtyard dining. The kind of place that appears in travel writing and then gets busy. Currently still manageable.
Best for: the evening nobody planned. Show up. Eat extremely well.
Worth the trip
To Eklekton Cypriot Corner
★★★★★
Off the tourist trail · Open until 3am · Locals only, basically
Popular with locals, overlooked by tourists — the combination that makes a restaurant worth finding. Meze, lamb, keftedes, lemon chicken soup, house wine. Generous portions, reasonable prices, late kitchen. If you find yourselves hungry at midnight, this is the answer.
The backup plan that turns into the favourite.
Beach lunch
Coral Bay tavernas
★★★★
Coral Bay · Lunch only · Don't overthink it
Several options along the beach. Don't research. Walk in, sit where there's shade, order halloumi, souvlaki, and cold Keo. They're all roughly equivalent. The beach does most of the work.
Pick one. Any one. This is not the meal you came to Cyprus for.
Since 1951
Hondros Tavern
★★★★★
Near Paphos Harbour · Open daily noon–10:30pm · Walk from centre
The oldest taverna in Paphos. Been going since 1951 and hasn't felt the need to change much, which is the point. Souvlaki cooked on an open charcoal pit as you walk in — you smell it before you see it. Kleftiko, meze, grilled octopus, roast potatoes done properly. Locals eat here. Tourists largely walk past it on the way to somewhere worse.
One group went twice in four days. That's the review.
Friday & Saturday nights
Konia Tavern
★★★★★
Konia village · 10 min taxi · Live music Fri & Sat · Book ahead
Ten minutes outside Paphos in Konia village, which is exactly why tourists don't find it. Live band on Fridays and Saturdays — locals dancing, not performing. Twenty-course meze, charcoal-grilled meats, lamb chops, loukoumades to finish. One reviewer said: "the locals are drinking and dancing and genuinely enjoying themselves — this is not a show for tourists." That is the test and this passes it.

For a group of nine with a Friday or Saturday night free, this is probably the best night of the trip.
Go on a Friday or Saturday. Book in advance. Take two taxis.
Match HQ
Keg & Barrel
★★★★★
Agiou Antoniou St · Bar Street entrance · Sports screens · Sponsor of Paphos Tigers RFC
Your base of operations for every match. Multiple large screens. Good beer selection. Welcoming to a group of nine men who have opinions about formations. Gets busy for big games — arrive early, claim territory, do not cede it.
Not a choice. A default state.
Match backup
Harry O's
★★★★
Paphos harbour area · Sports bar · Reliable
Overflow from Keg & Barrel when seating is full, or preferred some nights depending on the match and the mood. Not a downgrade. The beer is the same. The screens work.
The plan B that feels like plan A after one pint.
Old Town cocktails
Rum & Jam
★★★★★
Old Town alleyway · Small · Cocktail bar · Friendly staff
Tucked down a side alley in the Old Town. Super-cool, laid-back atmosphere, genuinely good cocktails. The kind of bar you find by accident and then return to on purpose. Works for a nightcap after dinner or as the start of an Old Town evening.
The one everyone says they'll go back to. Go back to it.
200 gins. No, really.
Ambience Gin House
★★★★
Paphos Old Town · Indoor · Designer interior · Trendy
Over 200 gins. This is the bar for the night someone decides the group is having a sophisticated evening. They are right. It works. The lighting is good. Nobody will admit enjoying it quite as much as they do.
Better than it sounds. Which is saying something, given it sounds excellent.
Sunset views
Piedra Cafe-Lounge Bar
★★★★
Edge of Old Town · Outdoor · Views over Paphos + sea
Cocktail and shisha bar with a genuine view over Paphos and out to sea. Good for sundowners before dinner when someone decides the group should do something slightly classy. They are correct. Stay an hour, then eat.
The 6:30pm decision that makes the evening start well.
Late night chaos — optional
Bar Street (Agiou Antoniou)
★★★
Kato Paphos · British-friendly · Loud · You know what this is
The main strip. Catering enthusiastically to British tourists since before most of you would care to admit. Karaoke. DJs. Sticky floors. Use sparingly — once or twice, for the football overflow or the night someone decides the group needs to go "somewhere with a proper atmosphere." They are not wrong. They are also not right.
Two visits maximum. You'll get one good story and one very clear reason to stop.
Default
The Harbour
Where most evenings start and many end. Food, drinks, walkability, sea views, and enough bars that nobody has to commit to a plan. The group gravitates here naturally even when attempting to decide somewhere else. Go with it.
The smart night
Paphos Old Town
Wine bars, cocktail bars, good restaurants, stone-walled everything. Go here when someone suggests "something a bit different tonight." They are right. The Old Town rewards the effort and is only a short taxi from the harbour. Rum & Jam, Ambience, Piedra, Mandra Tavern — it's all here.
Limited exposure
Bar Street
Formally: Agiou Antoniou Street. Informally: where Keg & Barrel is, where the football goes, where the night occasionally ends up. Fine in controlled doses. Not a lifestyle.

Nicosia: too far. Ayia Napa: wrong demographic energy. Larnaca: low payoff. Limassol: yes, once — marina lunch, walking, drinks, return same day. That's the full day trip list.

Structural anchor
Football
Football is not entertainment on this trip. It is the organisational framework. Everything else works around it.
Home base
Keg & Barrel
Default match venue. Multiple screens, good beer, seating that becomes strategic the moment anyone sits down. Arrive before the match starts. This is not optional.
Home base
Harry O's
Backup. Not a downgrade. Sometimes preferred. The group will develop a position on this by Day 3.
Tactical analysis
Increases in precision after the second beer. By the third, the manager is wrong. By the fourth, you'd have won it 3–0 with a completely different system that is explained at length.
The referee
Universally corrupt. This is agreed upon regardless of which team benefits from any given decision. Consistency is the one thing the group manages efficiently.
Seating
Strategic from the moment the group walks in. Whoever arrives first establishes position. Moving mid-match is not done. If the screen angle is wrong, this is mentioned, not resolved.

If a match conflicts with a dinner reservation, the match takes precedence. The reservation is "adjusted." Everyone knew this when they made it.

Greek Cypriot
Phrasebook
Real phrases. Accurate usage. British pronunciations — as in, how you'd actually say it after three pints rather than how a language app would have you say it. Tap each card for the full story.
Γεια μας
YA mas
Rhymes with "car jazz" but shorter. Just say "ya mas" and mean it.
Cheers / To us
Literal: "To our health." Used at every glass, every round, every time someone arrives at the table. Cannot be overused. The locals use it constantly and will warm to you immediately for trying.

Works for: toasts, arrivals, filling silences, acknowledging someone's round. Basically: whenever you'd raise a glass.
Use constantly. Zero risk of getting this wrong.
Άλλη μια μπύρα παρακαλώ
AL-ee MIA BEE-ra para-ka-LO
Say it like: "Alley mia beera, para-ca-lo." Stress the BEE. Do not stress the rest.
Another beer please
The full version — useful on Day 1 when you're still trying. By Day 3, "AL-ee mia" with a raised finger does the job. By Day 5, eye contact alone is sufficient. The bar staff adapt to your level of ambition faster than you do.

Advanced version: hold up however many fingers. "Tres" (TREH-ss) for three. They know.
Attempt the full version at least once. The effort is appreciated.
Πάμε
PA-meh
Like "Pammy" without the second syllable. Quick. Decisive.
Let's go / Come on
Universal motivator. Used for: taxis, extracting someone from a bar, football rallying, getting the group off sunbeds, general impatience with the pace of a decision-making process.

You'll hear it from locals during matches too — especially directed at players who are taking too long to do something obvious. You will understand exactly what it means.
Low risk. High utility. Use freely.
Ευχαριστώ
ef-ha-ri-STO
Think "eff-harry-STO". The "eff" is soft. Don't overthink it. Just say it.
Thank you
Basic. Essential. Universally well-received. Say it every time food arrives, which in Cyprus is frequently and in larger quantities than expected.

Locals notice the effort immediately and it costs you absolutely nothing. Most tourists don't bother. You're not most tourists.
Say it every time. No exceptions.
Σιγά σιγά
see-GA see-GA
Like "see-GAH see-GAH". Two identical syllables. Languid. Don't rush it — that would defeat the point.
Slowly, slowly — relax, no rush
The Cypriot national philosophy expressed in four syllables. Means: there is no urgency, you are rushing for no reason, another drink will appear, the problem will resolve itself or simply stop being a problem — which is functionally the same thing.

If you hear it directed at you by a local, it means you are behaving like someone who has forgotten they're on holiday. Stop it.

The group should adopt this as its operating system by Day 3.
Use on anyone who's being unnecessarily stressed. Including yourself.
Μαλάκας
ma-LA-kas
Stress the LA. Short, sharp. "Ma-LA-kas." Not "MAL-akas." The stress is important because you'll hear it said correctly constantly and want to match it.
Wanker / mate / you absolute idiot (depends entirely on tone)
Literal meaning: wanker. Real-world usage: Cyprus's most flexible word. Tone is everything.

Warm + grinning → "you legend, you complete and utter legend"
Flat + sighing → "you idiot, I can't believe you just did that"
Sharp + standing up → don't use it on this person right now

Cypriots use it as punctuation — between friends, constantly, with complete affection. You will hear it directed at the TV during matches, at waiters by other waiters, at friends greeting each other across the street.

Between your group: completely fine once the trip finds its feet. Directed at locals you've just met: hold off until you're reading the room accurately.
Tone is everything. Warm delivery only until you know the person.
Γαμώτο
gha-MO-to
The "gh" is a soft growl at the back of the throat — not quite a G, not quite an H. Think of the Scottish "loch" but voiced. Then "MO-to". Practice quietly before deploying.
For fuck's sake / bloody hell
General-purpose expletive. The go-to when something has gone wrong that didn't need to go wrong. Safe, widely understood, and proportionate.

Perfect for:
— A goal conceded in the 89th minute
— A referee decision of staggering ineptitude
— Someone ordering the wrong thing for the table
— Anyone suggesting a long walk at 11pm
— The taxi that was supposed to be here 15 minutes ago

Not directed at people. Used about situations. This distinction matters.
Sporting contexts only. Never at staff, never at locals.
Άντε γαμήσου
AN-deh gha-MEE-soo
Phonetically manageable. Conceptually: a hand grenade. Read the room before even attempting it.
Go fuck yourself
Not a greeting. Not a joke for bar staff. Not something to test on locals to see if they laugh. They may laugh. That does not mean it was a good idea.

Acceptable contexts:
— Said to a close friend who has just done something you find genuinely impressive and/or outrageous
— Muttered quietly at the television when a match takes a turn
— Read aloud in the villa as a pronunciation exercise that goes no further

If you're considering using it with someone you met two hours ago: don't.
Not for public use. Not for staff. Not for testing. Not for fun.
Πού είναι η τουαλέτα;
POO EE-neh ee too-ah-LEH-ta
"Poo ee-neh ee too-ah-LEH-ta." Yes, it starts with "poo." Maintain composure. This is adult travel.
Where is the toilet?
More useful than it sounds. Cyprus is a civilised country with accessible facilities, but in older tavernas and village restaurants things can be tucked away. Worth knowing.

Alternative approach: look for a man who works there and use a facial expression. This also works. But the phrase is better.
Opens with "poo." Power through. This is necessary.
Λογαριασμό παρακαλώ
lo-gha-ree-az-MO para-ka-LO
"Lo-garee-az-MO, para-ca-lo." Slightly long. Worth knowing because miming signing a bill starts to feel undignified by Day 4.
The bill, please
Useful. Dignified. Saves the awkward mime-signing routine that everyone does and nobody enjoys. Also: Cypriot restaurants do not rush you. You will need to ask. They are operating on σιγά σιγά time.

Once you've said it, the bill will arrive in three to eight minutes. This is normal. Order one more drink.
Use it. It's what the phrase was invented for.
Group dynamics
The nine archetypes
Every group of nine British men on holiday contains all of these. Identification usually takes until Day 2, though some reveal themselves at the airport.
The Researcher
Has read everything
Arrives with a spreadsheet, three restaurant recommendations with TripAdvisor rankings, a preferred jeep safari operator, and opinions about the microclimate in June. Has done more preparation than is necessary for a week of sitting by a pool. Is usually right about the restaurants.
"I looked this place up — actually, the one two streets over has better ratings and the kleftiko is apparently outstanding."
The Decisive One
Always suggests something immediately
Names the first restaurant within 30 seconds of the question being asked. Gets overruled. Forty minutes later, the group ends up exactly where he suggested. He does not say anything. He does not need to.
"What about that place by the harbour?" [every night, from Day 1]
The Flexible One
Genuinely open to anything
Says "I'm easy" with complete sincerity. Will quietly veto three options before anything is agreed. This is not obstruction — it is genuine preference avoidance, which turns out to have identical practical consequences.
"Whatever everyone else wants. I genuinely don't mind."
The Logistics Captain
Knows where everyone is
Books the taxi. Counts heads before leaving. Quietly texts the straggler. Receives no credit, which he expects. Essential to everything working, which he also knows. The trip collapses without him and nobody realises until Day 4.
"Right, shall we go? Where's [name]? Is he still at the bar?"
The Tactician
Football expert, post-beer edition
Analysis sharpens after the second drink. Formation opinions harden after the third. By the fourth, the manager is not just wrong but arguably negligent and possibly in breach of his contractual obligations. Could have won this in the first half.
"If they'd pressed higher from the start, this doesn't happen. It's basic."
The Moderate
Definitely not drinking tonight
States this clearly at 11am with full conviction. Is drinking by 6:30pm, somehow. Does not acknowledge the contradiction. Nobody brings it up. This is the correct social response and shows the group at its best.
"I'm just having the one. Early night for me. I mean it this time."
The Optimist
Already planning the next one
Says "we should do this every year" on Day 3. Says it again on Day 5. On Day 7 it becomes a formal proposal with provisional dates. Has said this before. The next trip is always being discussed before anyone has landed home.
"Genuinely though — same time next year? Greece? Sardinia? Open to discussion."
The Quiet Expert
Knows things without announcing it
Orders something in broken Greek and it works. Finds the local wine that costs half the price and tastes better. Asks the waiter something quiet that results in a better table. Says nothing about any of this. It simply happens around him.
[To waiter, quietly, in a combination of Greek and gestures that somehow works perfectly]
The Wild Card
Still going at 3am. Fine at breakfast.
Completely normal at dinner. Completely normal at the first bar. Normal through midnight. Appears at the villa pool at 8am having "not really slept, to be honest." Functional. Slightly alarming. Manages the jeep trip without complaint. Nobody fully understands this and nobody asks.
"I feel great, actually. I think the heat suits me."
